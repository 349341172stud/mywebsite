Section One

Reverend Insanity — Chapter 100

Quote

The setting sun slowly dropped below the mountain top, giving out the last ray of warm light. This light would be diminished by the unevenly criss-crossed branches of the withering trees, becoming the fragments of time scattered upon the green jade walls of bamboo houses.

“Oh no, my toy,” a girl cried out, chasing her spinning top among the crowd.

The spinning top rolled to Fang Yuan's feet, and the girl also hit Fang Yuan's leg, falling on the ground.

“Sorry, sorry! For offending lord Gu Master, please pardon us!” The little girl's father hurried over. When he saw Fang Yuan's clothing, his expression was white as paper, bringing the little girl and kneeling down, kowtowing to Fang Yuan.

The girl cried out of shock, pearls of tears trailing down her white and rosy face.

The walking mortals upon seeing this scene, turned away and avoided them like the plague.

Some Gu Masters stared coldly, before turning away and leaving.

“Stop crying, you troublemaker!” The father was frightened, angry and afraid, moving his hand to give her a slap, but Fang Yuan grabbed his hand, not allowing any movement.

“Just a small matter, no worries.” Fang Yuan laughed lightly, touching the little girl's head, softly consoling, “Don't be afraid, it is okay.”

The girl stopped crying, using her teary eyes to look at Fang Yuan, thinking this brother was really gentle.

“Thank you lord, thank you lord for your kindness!” The girl's father was overjoyed, kowtowing to Fang Yuan without a break.

Fang Yuan continued forward. His rented house was not far away. And on the ground floor of that bamboo house, his uncle Gu Yue Dong Tu stood, staring at Fang Yuan who was still a distance away, evidently waiting for him.

Analysis

There are three possible interpretations for this scene. First, Fang Yuan may be expressing his attitude of indifference towards small, insignificant matters by consoling the girl. This displays the vast insignificance and indifference he holds towards situations that do not benefit nor harm him.

Second, Fang Yuan may have had an urge to do this, so he did it. There are very prevalent examples of him acting based on urge when those actions express his emotions, such as poems like the one in Three Kings Inheritance, or any of his other poems. In Chapter 431, we are given explicit proof of this: “When my heart is evil, I slaughter all beings, and when I am virtuous, I bless the world. The world moves according to my emotions, I dominate everything, any enemy who resists me are trampled underfoot.”

Third, Fang Yuan’s consolation may be a narrative and symbolic parallel to the situation that unfolded soon after: heading to the internal affairs hall and meeting Gu Yue Dong Tu, his uncle, on the way. By showing his indifference towards this insignificant matter, he directly parallels the great benefit that was to occur soon after.

Section Two

I Want to Eat Your Pancreas

Analysis

The story follows the full course of a relationship between our two protagonists, Haruki Shiga and Sakura Yamauchi. Our story starts with Haruki going to the hospital to get stitches removed after his appendectomy, where he happens to come across a book titled Living With Dying in the waiting room. Moments later, when she catches him reading the book, we learn that the book belongs to a girl in his class, Sakura Yamauchi. She has terminal pancreatic cancer and has a few years left to live. Her book, Living With Dying, is her journal about her coexistence with her illness. Despite his overly calm and nonchalant reaction to her condition, she takes a liking to him, and a friendship blooms between them from that day to her unfortunate end.

The story deals with grief, loss, mortality, internal conflict, and discovering oneself. Two characters develop throughout the story, Haruki and Kyoko, with Haruki having more development. Haruki’s life was quite empty before meeting Sakura, and he himself admits that he had not been truly living before meeting Sakura. After their four months together, and after reading Living With Dying, Haruki has an epiphany and decides that he has truly lived in the past four months for the first time in his life. Haruki had never felt such emotion in his life, being incredibly joyful and happy, yet incredibly broken and sad. Shortly following his epiphany, he breaks down and cries the hardest he has ever cried before, with tears embodying happiness and sadness at the same time.

After regaining his composure, Haruki thanks Sakura’s mother and decides to share the rest of the messages Sakura left for people after she would die. Haruki passes along Sakura’s message to Kyoko, arranging for them to meet at the same cafe where he had been waiting for Sakura. Kyoko, after reading Sakura’s message, breaks down crying and declares that she will never forgive Haruki for not telling her about Sakura’s illness, then runs off. Haruki, deciding to keep his promise to Sakura to make friends with Kyoko, runs after her and asks that if she ever forgives him, they could become friends. Around a year after the main events of the story, we find out that Kyoko and Haruki have become friends, both visiting Sakura’s grave together and having lunch at Sakura’s mother’s house. Kyoko had grown from having something against Haruki and his relationship with Sakura to accepting their relationship and befriending Haruki.

The main themes of the story are mortality, discovering oneself, internal conflict, and dealing with grief and loss. Throughout their entire time together, Haruki and Sakura both deal with her mortality and how she will soon be dead. Sakura is deeply afraid of dying and hides her episodes by putting on a facade, while Haruki generally tries to occupy his mind with other things and spends his time with Sakura. One day at the hospital, Haruki asks Sakura if she will die. Sakura is confused by what he means, as she knows he is aware of her mortality. Haruki tells her that that’s not what he meant, and she quickly inquires if he wants her to keep living. The truth is that this entire time, Haruki is just as scared to lose Sakura, and coming to terms with her mortality would not occur within her lifetime.

After her death, Haruki has an epiphany and truly understands what it means to live. He finally comes to terms with Sakura’s mortality in the sense that he understands what she had lived for, and what he had lived for: to meet each other. Unfortunately for Sakura, her internal conflict regarding her fear of death had not been resolved, but Sakura’s life had meant something, and it allowed Haruki to be the one to end his internal conflicts regarding her mortality. He had discovered living, and through that, himself.

Early on, Sakura tells Haruki her philosophy: that every day is worth the same, as nobody is promised tomorrow. That is the author’s message: we should go out and live our lives, because no one is promised tomorrow. Haruki was not promised tomorrow, nor was Sakura. All Sakura had was a shortened estimate of her lifespan. Sakura did not die of her pancreatic ailment; she died because she was fatally stabbed. The core message is to live every day like it is your last, doing what makes you happy.

One extremely profound insight into life that I extracted from the story was the meaning of death. In the very beginning of the book, we learn of Sakura’s death, and the reader has no time to experience and read what she had lived for. After hundreds of pages of learning about Sakura, experiencing her through the lens of Haruki, and learning the meaning of living, we grow attached to the character. The message is simple: death itself does not make life valuable. It is what we do, how we live, the way we live, our actions, and our connections that give value to life. Death makes one appreciate life itself because we understand the value of what we have lost. This was one of the story’s ideas with the greatest impact on how I view the world, life, and myself.
